# JP Digital Works — Ideal Customer Profile

## Who They Are

**In one sentence:** Owner-operators of local service businesses with 2-18 employees who wear every hat and know they need better marketing and systems but have zero time to figure it out themselves.

**Situation:** Running a service business that's busy enough to be stressful but not big enough to have dedicated staff for marketing, admin, or technology. Revenue is decent but growth is capped by the owner's bandwidth.
**Identity:** They see themselves as hard workers who are great at their trade. They're not "tech people" and don't want to be — they want results, not tools to learn.
**Role:** They're the technician, the salesperson, the bookkeeper, the marketer, and the HR department. On a given day they're quoting jobs, managing employees, answering the phone, and trying to remember to post something on Facebook.
**Stage:** Established but plateaued — the business works, but scaling feels impossible without cloning themselves.

## Primary Pain

**The problem:** They're stuck doing everything manually — scheduling, invoicing, follow-ups, customer intake, social media — and it's eating the time they need to actually grow the business. Meanwhile, bigger competitors with bigger budgets are winning the marketing game.
**What it costs them:** 10-20 hours per week on tasks that could be automated. Missed leads (phone rings at 8pm, nobody answers). Lost visibility (no time for consistent SEO or social). Stalled growth because the owner IS the bottleneck.
**How they describe it:** "I know I should be doing more online but I just don't have the time." / "I'm too busy working IN the business to work ON the business." / "The big guys in town are everywhere online and I can't keep up."

**Secondary pains:**
- Wasting money on marketing that doesn't generate calls or leads
- Feeling behind on technology — they hear about AI but don't know where to start
- Can't find or afford good employees, so they do more themselves

## What They've Tried

| Alternative | Why it didn't work | What they learned |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Hired a marketing agency ($2-5k/month) | Expensive, slow to show results, generic strategies that didn't fit their business | "Marketing people don't understand my business" |
| DIY social media (posting themselves) | Inconsistent — does it for two weeks, gets busy, stops for three months | Posting isn't the problem — consistency and strategy are |
| Bought a website / ran Google Ads | Website doesn't rank, ads were expensive with unclear ROI | Throwing money at a website doesn't mean leads show up |
| Tried a CRM or software tool | Too complicated, didn't have time to learn it, stopped using it after a month | They need someone to set it up AND run it, not another login to manage |

**Sophistication level:** Intermediate — they've tried 1-2 things and are skeptical but still open if someone can show them it actually works.

## Desired Outcome

**What "after" looks like:** The business runs smoother. Leads come in automatically from Google and social. Follow-ups happen without the owner remembering to do them. Invoicing and scheduling aren't eating their evenings. The owner works 45 hours instead of 60 — and the business is growing faster than before.
**What they'd tell a friend:** "I finally got someone who actually gets AI and set up my whole system. I'm getting more calls and I'm not the one doing the posting or the follow-ups anymore."
**The deeper motivation:** Freedom. They started the business for independence, but it became a trap. They want a business that works for them — not the other way around. Some are thinking about eventually selling, and they know a business that depends entirely on the owner isn't sellable.

## Objections

| Objection | What's really behind it | How to address |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------|
| "I've been burned by marketing people before" | Paid for services that didn't deliver measurable results | Show specific, measurable outcomes. Offer a small first project to prove it works before committing big. |
| "I'm not a tech person" | Fear of being overwhelmed by tools they don't understand | Emphasize that they don't need to learn anything — JP Digital Works handles everything, they just see results |
| "I can't afford another expense right now" | Budget anxiety — they're used to marketing being a cost, not an investment | Frame it as cost savings (automate $X of labor) and lead generation (brings in $Y in new business). Show the math. |
| "AI is just hype / I don't trust it" | Heard too many promises about tech that didn't deliver | Show real examples from similar businesses. Let them see it working before committing. |

## Their Language

**Words they use for the problem:**
- "I'm wearing too many hats" — universal across all service industries
- "I don't have time for marketing" — framing marketing as separate from their real work
- "The big companies are eating our lunch" — competitive pressure from larger players
- "I need more leads / more calls" — they think in terms of phone calls and job bookings, not "conversions"
- "I'm doing everything myself" — exhaustion and frustration

**Words they use for the desired outcome:**
- "I just want the phone to ring" — leads = phone calls for service businesses
- "Get me on the first page of Google" — SEO framed in simple, visible terms
- "I want to stop doing [task] manually" — automation expressed as removing a specific burden
- "I want my business to run without me" — the deeper freedom play

**Words that would turn them off:**
- "Digital transformation" — too corporate, too vague
- "Synergize" / "optimize your funnel" — marketing jargon that signals "I don't understand your world"
- "Scalable AI infrastructure" — sounds expensive and complicated
- "Content strategy" — they don't think in these terms; they think "posting on Facebook"

**Jargon level:** None — use plain English for everything. They say "Google ranking" not "SERP position." They say "getting more calls" not "improving conversion rates."

## Where They Hang Out

| Platform/Channel | How they use it | Why it matters |
|-----------------|----------------|---------------|
| Facebook | Personal use, local business groups, community pages | Where they see what competitors are doing and where they'd post if they had time |
| Google Search | Looking for solutions when problems get bad enough | "How to get more customers for my [trade] business" — capture intent here |
| YouTube | Watch how-to videos when trying to learn something themselves | Opportunity for educational content that builds trust |
| Local networking / BNI / Chamber of Commerce | In-person referrals and relationships | Word of mouth is still the #1 way they find service providers |
| Nextdoor / local Facebook groups | Monitoring reputation, asking for recommendations | Where their customers are recommending (or not recommending) them |

**People they trust:** Other local business owners, their accountant, their spouse/partner, industry-specific trade associations. They trust peers over experts.

## Buying Triggers

What pushes them from "considering" to "buying":
- A competitor visibly winning online — new truck, bigger crew, more Google reviews — and they know it's because of better marketing
- Losing a big job to a competitor who showed up first on Google
- Reaching a breaking point with manual work — "I can't keep doing this myself"
- Hearing a peer or fellow business owner rave about results from AI or automation
- Seeing a specific, relatable example from their own industry (not a generic case study)

## Secondary Segments

### Segment: The Growth-Ready Scaler
- **Who:** Service business at 15-30 employees, has some systems in place, ready to add AI to scale further
- **Key difference from primary:** Has more budget, less skepticism, needs more sophisticated automation (multi-location, team workflows)
- **Priority:** Medium — higher deal value but smaller volume in the market

### Segment: The New Business Launcher
- **Who:** Just started a service business (under 2 years), wants to get online presence right from the start
- **Key difference from primary:** Less established, tighter budget, more open to new technology, less baggage from bad agency experiences
- **Priority:** Low — lower deal value, but good for building reputation and case studies
