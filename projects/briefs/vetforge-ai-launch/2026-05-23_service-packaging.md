## Pricing Philosophy

Four decisions shape everything below:

1. **Cheaper than the alternatives, on purpose.** A vet marketing agency runs $1,500–5,000/mo. A part-time marketing hire runs $2,000–3,000/mo plus the management headache. Entry starts at **$600/mo** — low enough that a buyer-group peer recommends it without a single objection about price.
2. **A service, not a subscription.** No login, no dashboard, no "platform." The client gets a person who does the work and sends results. The website and domain stay in the client's name — this is a service relationship, not a hostage situation.
3. **AI is the margin.** Claude does the heavy lifting on the backend, so we deliver agency-level output at a fraction of agency labor. ~2–5 hours of hands-on time per client per month after setup — which makes low pricing profitable and a solo operation scalable.
4. **We operate, we don't just advise.** The leverage is in *doing the work, not handing over a report.* When we maintain the website ourselves, our recommendations actually ship, the client never has to manage anything, and leaving means migrating their site — retention that comes from value, not contracts.

**The buyer-group test:** every price and tier name has to survive being said out loud at a VMG peer meeting. "We're on the Forge plan, twelve hundred a month, he runs our whole website and keeps us showing up in Google *and* ChatGPT" should sound like a steal.

## The AI-Search Moat (why maintenance is recurring, not one-time)

Classic Google SEO has a relatively stable playbook. AI search — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, Google's AI Overviews — does **not**. The rules shift constantly, and there is no "set it and forget it":

- **Structured data / schema** that tells AI exactly what the practice is, where, and what it offers
- **AI-crawler access** (`llms.txt`, robots rules) so the practice's content is eligible to be cited at all
- **Content written to be retrieved and cited** — clear entities, plain-language Q&A, strong local signals (city, services, hours)
- **Citation & NAP consistency** across the web so AI trusts the practice as an entity
- **Monitoring the actual answers** — when a pet owner asks "best vet near me" or "emergency vet in [city]," are *they* the answer, or the corporate chain?

That instability is the asset. "We keep up with how AI search works so you don't have to, and we keep you in the answer" is a promise that *has to be ongoing*. We're not selling maintenance — we're selling **staying found in a target that keeps moving**, in both legacy Google SEO and the AI engines that are eating search.

## How We Work — Your Site, Your Say

Operating your website doesn't mean operating *over your head*. Three principles keep you in control — and apply across every tier:

**1. We report on everything.** Even though we do the work, you see the work. Every month you get a plain-English report: what we changed, where you stand in Google *and* AI search, what moved, and what's next. No black box — that transparency is the difference between a service and a subscription you forget you're paying for.

**2. You have the final say.** Pick your comfort level and change it any time:
- **Review-first** — for anything visible (new pages, content, design changes) we send a preview link; nothing goes live until you approve. Routine technical SEO (schema, metadata, speed) happens automatically and shows up in your report.
- **Auto-pilot** — we make changes and report after.

Most practices start on Review-first and switch to Auto-pilot once they trust the work. (Handoff clients approve with their own developer; we hand over the specs.)

**3. Post it yourself when you want to.** Want to put up a blog, a staff announcement, or a new service page? No login, no CMS. Write it, drop the file in your shared VetForge folder, and we publish it for you — run through your brand voice profile and optimized for Google + AI search before it goes live. **You write; we make it shine and ship it.** (Operator tiers publish it directly; for Handoff clients we optimize it and pass it to your developer to post.)

## Client Onboarding Process

Built around the buyer-group referral motion, with a fork for contract-locked practices.

1. **Discovery call (free, 30 min).** Understand the practice. Run a free GEO/SEO + AI-search snapshot live on their site — show the gaps in real time, including whether they currently show up in AI answers. **Ask: "Are you under contract with a web developer, and when's it up?"** — this routes them to Handoff vs Operator.
2. **Free snapshot delivered.** Short, concrete doc of what's broken and what's possible. Even non-signers remember it and tell peers.
3. **Tier recommendation + proposal.** Recommend a single tier:
   - Locked into a dev contract → **Spark (Handoff)**, with the upgrade path spelled out for when the contract ends.
   - Free to switch, decent site → **Forge (Operator)**.
   - Outdated/DIY site → **Website Rebuild → Forge/Foundry**.
4. **Sign + invoice.** Month-to-month agreement; $500 setup (or rebuild project) invoiced.
5. **Onboarding intake (Week 1).** Build the brand voice profile, get GBP access (and site/dev access for Operator tiers), run the baseline audit. **Set their approval preference** (Review-first vs Auto-pilot — default to Review-first) and **set up their shared content folder** so they can drop blogs/updates for us to publish.
6. **First deliverables (by end of Week 2).** Baseline audit + first content batch. For Operator tiers, first round of implemented site changes. Fast first value is what they tell their buyer group about.
7. **Monthly cadence begins.** Predictable schedule. Each happy client is the next referral.

## Go-to-Market Reality (the early stretch)

A few hard truths about the first clients, learned from the HPAH beta:

- **Most early clients will be Handoff (Spark), not Operator.** Practices worth having usually already have a web designer and a site they're reasonably happy with. Asking them to hand over the whole website on day one is a big ask and a slow yes. **Lead with Spark** — get in via SEO/AI-search + content + web-dev plans, prove value, earn trust.
- **The Website Rebuild is a long game, not a cold open.** Almost nobody buys a rebuild from someone they just met. It lands later, after months of delivered results.
- **The demo rebuild is your bridge.** Build a rebuilt version of a prospect's *own* site (AI-search-ready, improved) and show it to them. It makes "what's possible" concrete without asking them to change anything yet — and it's how you eventually earn the Operator/rebuild work. This is exactly what worked with HPAH: the demo showed the upside while their current site kept running.
- **Don't fight the incumbent web designer.** Work alongside them — you feed the plans, they build. When their contract or patience runs out, you're the obvious one to take over. Friction with the existing vendor kills referrals.

**The sequence:** Handoff (Spark) → trust + results → demo what's possible → Operator / rebuild when they're ready.

## How to Present Pricing (sales notes)

- **Lead with the alternative, not the price.** "An agency would charge three grand a month and not understand a vet practice. This is twelve hundred, and I built it inside one."
- **Anchor on involvement, not features.** Their #1 fear is another project on their plate. Sell the 15 minutes a month and "you never touch your website again."
- **You still see everything, and you still have the say.** "We do the work, but you get a report every month and nothing visible goes live without your okay. It's your site — we just run it for you." Kills the "I don't want to lose control" objection.
- **Post-it-yourself is a delight, not a burden.** "Want to throw up a blog about your new vet or a holiday closure? Just write it and drop it in your folder — we'll polish it and put it up, optimized so it actually helps you get found."
- **For the locked-in practice: don't make them fire anyone.** "You don't have to drop your web guy. We'll feed him exactly what to change and handle everything else. When that contract's up, we bring it all under one roof." Removes the biggest reason a referral stalls.
- **Sell the AI-search fear honestly.** "When a pet owner asks ChatGPT for a vet, you want to be the answer — not VCA down the road. That's not a thing you fix once; it's a thing we keep current."
- **Never present the full menu cold.** Recommend one tier. Menus paralyze a time-poor buyer.
- **Setup fee = commitment, not cost.** "It covers building your brand voice profile — the thing that makes all of this sound like you, not a robot."
- **Month-to-month is a feature.** "No contract. If it's not working, you walk. I keep your business by delivering, not by locking you in."
