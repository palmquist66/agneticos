I’m very sorry to hear that we upset you with our handling of your sweet pup. Our goal is to clearly communicate what we are doing and why, communication is the cornerstone of our practice. It is clear we fell short in your pup’s exam! Signs of anxiety can be very subtle but stand out clearly to our seasoned and professional team. Our goal is to avoid this entirely by offering lots of treats and encouragement from the time a pet enters our hospital (we call it coming to ‘grandma’s house’). In fact we’ve designed our entire practice around lowering anxiety; no waiting room, taking clients directly into an exam room (no other dog interactions), floor to ceiling windows in the exam rooms, and showering of treats and praise on pups all help lower anxiety. Most clients' pets come into the office wagging their tails in excitement because they know what is coming….TREATS. That said, pets showing anxiety aren’t doing anything ‘wrong’, in fact we appreciate when they send us clear indicators that they aren’t comfortable, it is easier to manage the visit. I have sent to you some information related to anxiety in exam rooms and actions that can/may be taken for future reference.



In reviewing our process and protocols it appears the circumstances of your visit were such that we didn’t send to you that information prior to your visit. You and your sweet pup fell between the groups we typically send this information to, those that have shown prior signs of anxiety and/or new to the owner pups. There are many methods to deal with anxious pets, one of the more effective is moving the client out of the immediate area and/or covering the dog's head. Often this method lowers the reactive nature of anxious pets which allows us to perform the necessary procedures without trauma. In the case of your pup it worked as intended, unfortunately you weren’t aware of the potential methods we employ. For that we apologize. We wish you and your pup the best.
