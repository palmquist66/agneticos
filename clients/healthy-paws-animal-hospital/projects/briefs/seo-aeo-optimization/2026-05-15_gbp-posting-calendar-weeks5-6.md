POST 13: Service Spotlight, Spay and Neuter
(Publish Monday or Tuesday)

Spaying or neutering your pet is one of the best decisions you can make for their long-term health. It's not just about preventing unwanted litters. Spaying reduces the risk of uterine infections and certain cancers in female dogs and cats. Neutering lowers the risk of prostate problems and testicular cancer in males. The health benefits are real.

At Healthy Paws, we perform spay and neuter surgeries right here in Lake in the Hills. We take the time before every procedure to walk you through what to expect, from pre-surgical bloodwork to recovery at home. You'll know exactly what's happening and why.

If your pet hasn't been spayed or neutered yet, or if you have a new puppy or kitten and want to plan ahead, give us a call. We'll help you decide on the right timing for your pet's age and breed.

POST 14: Pet Care Tip, Summer Heat and Pavement Safety
(Publish Wednesday or Thursday)

Summer in Lake in the Hills means temperatures that can make pavement dangerously hot for your dog's paws. On a 90-degree day, asphalt can reach 150 degrees or higher. That's hot enough to burn skin in seconds.

Here's a simple test: press the back of your hand flat against the pavement for seven seconds. If you can't hold it there comfortably, it's too hot for your dog to walk on. Move walks to early morning or after sunset, stick to grass when you can, and always bring water.

Watch for heavy panting, drooling, stumbling, or a bright red tongue. Those are signs of heat exhaustion and they need attention fast. Dogs cool down differently than we do, and some breeds overheat much more quickly than others.

If you're ever unsure whether your pet is handling the heat well, call us. We'd rather answer a quick question now than treat a heat emergency later.

POST 16: Service Spotlight, Gentle Handling and Fearful Pets
(Publish Monday or Tuesday)

Some pets are afraid of the vet. We understand that, and we take it seriously.

At Healthy Paws, we will not fight an animal to achieve our goals. That's a line we drew a long time ago. If your dog is trembling or your cat is hiding in the carrier, we slow down. We adjust. We figure out what your pet needs to feel safe before we start an exam.

Sometimes that means dimming the lights or letting a nervous cat settle in the room before we approach. Sometimes it means rescheduling a non-urgent procedure for a day when your pet is in a better place. We'd rather take our time and get it right than force a stressful experience that makes the next visit even harder.

If your pet has had a rough time at other clinics, or if vet visits have become something you both dread, we want you to know there's another way to do this.

Call us and tell us about your pet. We'll take it from there.

POST 17: Pet Care Tip, Summer Travel and Boarding Prep
(Publish Wednesday or Thursday)

Planning a trip this summer? Whether your dog is coming with you or staying with a sitter, a little prep goes a long way.

If your pet is boarding, most facilities require current vaccinations, including Bordetella for dogs. Don't wait until the week before your trip to find out your dog isn't up to date. Schedule a wellness check now so you're not scrambling later. We'll make sure their vaccinations are current and give you the documentation you need.

Traveling with your pet? Bring their regular food, any medications they're taking, and a copy of their health records. Motion sickness is more common in dogs than most people realize. If your dog gets anxious or nauseous in the car, talk to us beforehand. We can help.

And make sure your pet's microchip information is current. If your contact details have changed since you registered the chip, update them before you leave. A microchip only works if the information behind it is accurate.

POST 18: What "Privately Owned" Actually Means for Your Pet
(Publish Friday or Saturday)

You'll see "privately owned" on our website and in our lobby. Here's why we think it matters.

When a veterinary practice is privately owned, the vet who sees your pet is the same person making decisions about how the practice runs. That's us. We decide how long appointments last. We decide what products to carry based on what works, not what a distributor is pushing. And we keep our lobby small and do checkout in the exam room because that's better for you and your pet, even though it's less efficient on paper.

Corporate-owned practices answer to a home office. Their vet may want to spend more time with your pet, but the schedule says otherwise. We don't have that problem. The people in the room are the people in charge.
