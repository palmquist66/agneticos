## When Is a Pet Considered "Senior"?

One of the most common questions we hear from pet owners is when their pet officially becomes a senior. The answer depends on size and species:

- **Cats:** Generally considered senior around age 10-12
- **Small dogs** (under 20 lbs): Senior around age 10-12
- **Medium dogs** (20-50 lbs): Senior around age 8-10
- **Large dogs** (50-90 lbs): Senior around age 7-8
- **Giant breeds** (over 90 lbs): Senior as early as age 5-6

This means that a Great Dane at age 6 may have the same health considerations as a Chihuahua at age 11. At Healthy Paws Animal Hospital, we tailor our senior care recommendations to your specific pet rather than applying a one-size-fits-all approach.

## Why Twice-Yearly Exams Matter

We recommend senior pets visit us every six months rather than once a year. Here is why this matters so much:

**Pets age faster than we do.** A single year for a senior dog is roughly equivalent to 5-7 human years. Waiting 12 months between exams is like you seeing your doctor only every 5-7 years. A lot can change in that time.

**Pets hide illness.** This is an evolutionary survival instinct. By the time most pets show obvious symptoms, a disease may have been progressing for months. Regular exams and lab work catch changes when they are still manageable.

**Early detection saves lives — and money.** Conditions like kidney disease, diabetes, thyroid disorders, and certain cancers respond much better to treatment when caught early. Early intervention is also typically less expensive than emergency treatment of advanced disease.

## What a Senior Wellness Exam Includes

When your senior pet visits us, our exam goes beyond a quick listen to the heart. We take a thorough approach:

**Comprehensive physical exam.** We check everything from nose to tail — eyes, ears, dental health, lymph nodes, heart and lung sounds, abdominal palpation, joint mobility, skin condition, and body weight.

**Senior bloodwork panel.** This typically includes a complete blood count, chemistry panel (liver, kidneys, blood sugar, protein levels), thyroid screening, and urinalysis. These values create a baseline so we can identify trends over time.

**Blood pressure measurement.** Hypertension is common in senior cats and dogs and can damage organs silently.

**Body condition and weight assessment.** Even small weight changes in senior pets can indicate underlying health issues. We track trends over time.

**Pain assessment.** Arthritis and chronic pain are extremely common in aging pets but often go unrecognized. We evaluate mobility, muscle mass, and joint comfort.

## Common Senior Pet Health Issues

Through regular monitoring, we frequently catch these conditions early in our senior patients:

- **Chronic kidney disease** — especially common in cats, manageable with diet and fluids when caught early
- **Arthritis and degenerative joint disease** — treatable with multimodal pain management
- **Dental disease** — often accelerates in senior years and causes significant hidden pain
- **Thyroid disorders** — hyperthyroidism in cats, hypothyroidism in dogs
- **Cognitive dysfunction** — similar to dementia in humans, with available supportive therapies
- **Heart disease** — early detection allows medication to slow progression
- **Cancer** — earlier detection means more treatment options

## Helping Your Senior Pet at Home

Between visits, you can support your senior pet's health by:

- Maintaining a consistent, age-appropriate diet
- Keeping them at a healthy weight (extra pounds stress aging joints)
- Providing low-impact exercise appropriate to their ability
- Watching for changes in eating, drinking, bathroom habits, or energy level
- Making your home comfortable with ramps, orthopedic beds, and easy litter box access
