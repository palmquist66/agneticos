## What Does "Low-Stress Veterinary Care" Mean?

If your pet trembles in the car on the way to the vet, hides under furniture when they see the carrier, or becomes aggressive during examinations, you are not alone. Veterinary anxiety is one of the most common concerns pet owners share with us — and it is exactly why our entire team at Healthy Paws Animal Hospital is trained in gentle, low-stress handling techniques.

Our approach is built around reducing fear, anxiety, and stress (FAS) in pets during every interaction. It is not just a philosophy — it is a deliberate methodology backed by behavioral science that changes how we handle every aspect of your pet's visit.

## Why Reducing Veterinary Stress Matters

Stress during veterinary visits is not just unpleasant — it has real medical consequences:

**Stressed pets are harder to examine accurately.** Elevated heart rate, tense muscles, and defensive postures can mask symptoms or create false positives during physical exams.

**Anxiety escalates over time.** Each stressful visit builds on the last. Pets develop stronger negative associations, making future visits progressively more difficult.

**Owners delay care.** When pet owners dread bringing their pet to the vet, they may postpone important preventive visits. This leads to conditions being caught later than they should be.

**Quality of life.** Your pet deserves healthcare that does not cause emotional trauma. We believe strongly that veterinary medicine should never be something your pet fears.

## How Our Gentle Approach Works

At Healthy Paws Animal Hospital, low-stress principles shape every part of your pet's experience:

### Before the Visit

- We offer pre-visit calming recommendations tailored to your pet's anxiety level
- We suggest bringing your pet's favorite treats, a familiar blanket, or a beloved toy
- Cat owners can prepare carriers early so they become familiar rather than frightening

### The Waiting Room (or Lack Thereof)

One of our core design principles is that **your pet goes directly to an exam room** — no sitting in a crowded lobby surrounded by other anxious animals. We consider it a failure if you spend time in our waiting area. This single change eliminates one of the biggest stress triggers for both dogs and cats.

### During the Exam

- **Gentle handling techniques** — we use minimal restraint and allow pets to choose comfortable positions
- **Treat rewards throughout** — positive associations are built one treat at a time
- **Pheromone diffusers** in exam rooms to promote calm (Feliway for cats, Adaptil for dogs)
- **Non-slip surfaces** so pets feel secure on exam tables
- **Reading body language** — our team is trained to recognize early signs of stress and adjust immediately before anxiety escalates
- **Going at your pet's pace** — we pause, redirect, and modify our approach based on how your pet is feeling

### For Cats Specifically

Cats experience veterinary stress differently than dogs, and we have specific protocols:

- Exam room lighting can be dimmed
- We allow cats to remain in the bottom half of their carrier during initial examination
- Towel wrapping provides a sense of security
- We minimize the number of people in the room
- Cat-specific pheromones are always present

## What You Can Do to Help

Your calm energy matters more than you might realize. Here are ways to support your pet:

- **Stay relaxed.** Pets pick up on your anxiety. If you are nervous, they will be too.
- **Practice car rides.** Short, positive car trips that do not end at the vet help reduce travel anxiety.
- **Carrier training for cats.** Leave the carrier out at home with treats and bedding inside. Make it a safe space, not a trap.
- **Bring high-value treats.** Ask us what is safe for your pet — we want them associating our office with good things.

Pet owners who switch to our practice from traditional veterinary offices consistently tell us the difference is dramatic. Pets who once had to be sedated for basic exams are now walking in willingly. Cats who hid for days after vet visits now recover within minutes of returning home.

This is not accidental — it is the result of deliberate training that our entire team has completed and continues to practice every day.
