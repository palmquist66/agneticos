## Why Dental Health Matters More Than You Think

February is National Pet Dental Health Month, and our team at Healthy Paws Animal Hospital wants to share something that surprises many pet owners: by age three, most dogs and cats already have some degree of periodontal disease. This is not just about bad breath — untreated dental disease can affect your pet's heart, kidneys, and liver.

We see the impact of dental disease every day in our practice. Pets who seem perfectly healthy on the outside may be living with significant oral pain that they instinctively hide. As their veterinary team, we consider dental care one of the most important aspects of your pet's overall wellness.

## Signs of Dental Disease to Watch For

Your pet cannot tell you their mouth hurts, but they often show subtle signs:

- **Bad breath** that persists (this is not normal — it indicates bacteria)
- **Red, swollen, or bleeding gums**
- **Difficulty eating** or dropping food while chewing
- **Pawing at the mouth** or rubbing their face
- **Drooling more than usual**
- **Loose or discolored teeth**
- **Reluctance to chew toys** they previously enjoyed

Many pet owners attribute these changes to aging, but in our experience, addressing the underlying dental issue often reveals a much happier, more energetic pet.

## What Happens During a Professional Dental Cleaning

A professional veterinary dental cleaning is quite different from what happens at a groomer or what you can accomplish at home. Here is what our process looks like:

**Pre-anesthetic bloodwork.** We always run lab work before anesthesia to ensure your pet is a safe candidate. This is especially important for senior pets.

**Full oral exam under anesthesia.** Your pet needs to be asleep for us to thoroughly examine every tooth, the gum line, and the tissues in the back of the mouth. Awake exams miss a significant amount.

**Digital dental radiographs.** Just like your dentist takes X-rays, we radiograph your pet's teeth. Up to 60% of dental disease hides below the gum line where we cannot see it. Radiographs reveal root abscesses, bone loss, and other problems invisible to the naked eye.

**Ultrasonic scaling and polishing.** We remove tartar above and below the gum line, then polish the teeth to create a smooth surface that resists future plaque accumulation.

**Extractions if needed.** When teeth are diseased beyond saving, removing them eliminates a source of chronic pain and infection. Pets do remarkably well after extractions — most owners report their pet acts years younger.

## What You Can Do at Home

Professional cleanings are essential, but daily home care slows the progression of dental disease between visits:

- **Brush daily** if possible. Even a few times per week helps. Use pet-specific toothpaste (never human toothpaste).
- **Dental chews and treats** approved by the Veterinary Oral Health Council (VHOC) provide some mechanical cleaning action.
- **Water additives** can help reduce bacterial load in the mouth.
- **Regular check-ups** allow us to catch problems before they become painful or expensive.
